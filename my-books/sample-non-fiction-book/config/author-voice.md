# Author Voice

This file describes the unique qualities of the author's style and voice that must be preserved during editing and proofreading.

---

## What is the "author's voice"?

**Author's voice** is the distinctive manner of writing that makes text recognizable. For this crypto trading book, the voice is established through the Introduction and Chapter 1 (~4,700 words analyzed).

**Important**: During editing and proofreading the author's voice must be PRESERVED, even if it deviates from standard norms.

---

**Tone**: First-person confessional, brutally honest, direct, no-nonsense

- [x] Conversational
- [x] Honest/confessional
- [x] Direct and blunt
- [ ] Formal
- [ ] Academic

**One-sentence description**:

## Lexical Features

### Preferred words and expressions

**Frequently used words**:
- "brutal" / "brutally" — emphasizing harsh truths
- "uncomfortable truth" — introducing difficult realities
- "Let me be clear" / "Let's be honest" — direct truth-telling
- "Here's what X actually means" — cutting through BS
- "the hard way" — learning through painful experience
- "systematically" vs "emotionally" — core framework distinction

**Signature expressions**:
- "I learned this the hard way" — personal credibility
- "Want to guess how that went?" — rhetorical, conversational
- "Let me show you what X looks like" — teaching through examples
- "This isn't sexy. It should." — anti-hype, reality check
- "None of this is about intelligence" — removing ego from equation
- "Tuition fee" — cost of learning through losses (recurring metaphor)

**Words to avoid**:
- "Revolutionary" / "game-changing" — crypto hype language
- "To the moon!" / "HODL!" / "Wen lambo?" — crypto slang undermines credibility
- Overly technical jargon without explanation — accessibility matters
- "Easy" / "guaranteed" / "sure thing" — false promises

### Formality level

- [ ] High (formal, complex constructions)
- [x] **Medium-low** (conversational but structured)
- [ ] Low (slang-heavy, very casual)

**Examples**:
```
Formal: "One must exercise prudence when allocating capital"
Author's voice: "If you trade the way most people trade—impulsively, frequently, without risk management—you will lose. Statistically, you will lose."
```

## Syntactic Features

### Sentence length

**Typical length**:
- [x] **Mixed** — for rhythm and impact
  - Short punchy sentences for emphasis (3-8 words)
  - Medium for explanation (15-25 words)
  - Occasional longer for storytelling (30+ words)

**Example of typical rhythm**:
> "Gone. Six months of disciplined trading. Six months of small, consistent wins. Erased in ninety seconds because I broke every rule I thought I understood."

(Notice: One word. Then two parallel sentences. Then the payoff explanation.)

### Sentence structure

**Preferred structure**:
- [x] Simple sentences for emphasis
- [x] **Parcelling** (intentional fragments) — "Gone." "Simple math." "Repeat."
- [x] Parallel construction for rhythm
- [x] Rhetorical questions followed by direct answers

**Examples**:
```
Fragment for impact: "Gone."
Parallel structure: "I made $12,000 in my first month... I lost $15,000 in my second month... I made $8,000 in month three..."
Rhetorical Q&A: "Want to guess how that went? I lost money on 60% of the trades..."
```

## Rhythm and Pace

### Pacing

**Overall pace**:
- [x] **Variable** (changes by situation)
  - Fast: personal disaster stories, emotional trading scenarios
  - Slow: framework explanations, systematic breakdowns
  - Builds tension then releases with blunt truth

**Rhythm creation techniques**:
- **Rule of three**: "Six months. Six months. Erased in ninety seconds."
- **List acceleration**: Short items building to longer payoff
- **Punch-line structure**: Setup → setup → blunt reveal
  > "The second month, I lost $15,000. The third month, I made $8,000 back. The fourth month, I lost $22,000."

### Pauses and accents

**Use of pauses**:
- **Em-dashes**: For aside commentary ("—and the worst part?")
- **Short fragment sentences**: Create full stops for emphasis ("Gone.")
- **Rhetorical questions**: Force reader pause before answer
- **Paragraph breaks**: After major reveals for impact

**Example**:
> "I was playing a game where the house wins whether I do or not."
(Full paragraph. Full stop. Let it land.)

## Stylistic Devices

### Favorite Techniques

**Metaphors and comparisons**:
- Type: Concrete, relatable, often financial/gaming
- Examples:
  > "Trading is a zero-sum game (actually, it's worse)"
  > "I was playing a game where the house wins whether I do or not"
  > "Tuition fee" — cost of learning through losses
  > "like in a casino" — familiar comparison

**Repetition**:
- **Anaphora** (repetition at start): Used for emphasis
  > "I didn't sell because... I didn't sell because... I didn't sell because..."

- **Parallel structure**: Lists of similar failures/successes
  > "Scenario 1: FOMO Buying / Scenario 2: Revenge Trading / Scenario 3: Diamond Hands Delusion"

**Rhetorical devices**:
- **Contrast**: Emotional vs systematic, winners vs losers
- **Direct address**: "Let me show you..." "Want to guess..."
- **Confession then lesson**: Personal failure → extracted wisdom

## Specific Numbers and Concrete Details

### Financial Specificity

**Key characteristic**: Uses exact dollar amounts, not vague "a lot"
- "$47,000 lesson" (title of intro)
- "$63,000 to $16,000 in ninety seconds"
- "I made forty-seven trades in five days"
- "paid over $800 in fees"
- "ended the week down $3,200"

**Why it matters**:
- Creates credibility (real experience)
- Makes losses tangible, not abstract
- Shows stakes are real

### Temporal Precision

- "3:47 AM"
- "exactly thirty-two days"
- "ninety seconds"
- "fourteen hours straight"

**Effect**: Adds authenticity, these are real memories

## Structural Patterns

### Chapter Opening Pattern

1. **Personal story** (specific, dollar amounts, failure)
2. **Lesson learned** (what was missing)
3. **Transition to teaching** (here's what you need to know)

**Example** (Chapter 1):
- Opens with $12k win → losses
- "I was missing something fundamental"
- "Let's start with the numbers you probably don't want to hear"

### Section Pattern

1. **Bold assertion** (often uncomfortable truth)
2. **Personal example** (I did this wrong)
3. **Framework explanation** (here's the right way)
4. **Contrast** (emotional vs systematic)

### Chapter Closing Pattern

1. **Summary of key point** (bold, blunt)
2. **Path forward** (but here's the good news...)
3. **Transition to next chapter** (natural progression)

## Deliberate Deviations from Norms

### Grammatical Liberties

**Permitted deviations**:

1. **Sentence fragments for emphasis**
   - Example: "Gone." "Simple math." "Repeat."
   - Why preserve: Creates punch, mirrors emotional impact

2. **Starting sentences with "And" or "But"**
   - Example: "And the worst part?" "But here's the good news:"
   - Why preserve: Conversational flow, emphasis

3. **Em-dashes for asides**
   - Heavy use throughout for commentary
   - Example: "—myself included, for a long time—"
   - Why preserve: Conversational tone, thinking-out-loud quality

### Author-Specific Terms

- **"The house"** — exchanges and their economic advantage
- **"Playing a game"** — trading as probability system
- **"Tuition fee"** — cost of learning through losses (KEY METAPHOR)
- **"Diamond hands delusion"** — specific critique of crypto culture
- **"Survivorship bias"** — recurring analytical framework

## What to Avoid

### Taboo Words and Phrases

**Never use**:
- "To the moon!" / "HODL!" / "Wen lambo?" — crypto hype
- "This one simple trick" — clickbait language
- "Guaranteed returns" — false promises
- "Revolutionary new strategy" — hype
- Excessive emojis or crypto slang — maintains seriousness

### Unacceptable Techniques

**Avoid**:
- Sugarcoating losses — be specific and honest
- Promising easy money — contradicts core message
- Technical jargon without explanation — accessibility matters
- Removing personal failures — credibility comes from honest losses

## Reference Samples

### Sample 1: Personal Story Opening

**Demonstrates**: Confessional tone, specific details, vulnerability

```
It was 3:47 AM when I watched my account balance drop from $63,000 to $16,000 in less than ninety seconds.

I wasn't asleep. I had been glued to my laptop for fourteen hours straight, watching Bitcoin swing wildly between $58,000 and $61,000. I had opened a 10x leveraged long position earlier that evening, convinced I was catching the "bottom" of a minor dip. The chart looked perfect. The indicators all lined up. Twitter was buzzing with bullish sentiment. This was it—my chance to turn a good month into a life-changing month.

Then Elon Musk tweeted something about Tesla's Bitcoin holdings, the price flash-crashed to $52,000, and my position was liquidated before I could even move my cursor to the "close trade" button.
```

**Key features**:
1. Specific time and dollar amounts
2. Physical details (glued to laptop, couldn't move cursor)
3. Builds tension then delivers punch
4. Shows thinking process leading to mistake

### Sample 2: Blunt Truth-Telling

**Demonstrates**: Direct address, no sugarcoating, framework delivery

```
Let me be very clear about what this book is and what it isn't.

**This is NOT**:
- A get-rich-quick manual
- A technical analysis masterclass
- A list of "hot coins" to buy
- A promise that you'll make life-changing money

**This IS**:
- A field guide written by someone who has paid the tuition fee many times over
- An honest account of what actually works (and what definitely doesn't)
- A systematic approach to trading crypto without destroying yourself
- A reality check about what it takes to survive long enough to become profitable
```

**Key features**:
1. Direct address ("Let me be clear")
2. Structured contrast (NOT vs IS)
3. Honest about limitations
4. "Tuition fee" — author-specific metaphor

### Sample 3: Framework Teaching

**Demonstrates**: Systematic explanation, contrast, practical examples

```
Now let's contrast that with systematic trading:

- You have a predefined entry strategy (e.g., "I only buy when X condition is met")
- You have a predefined position size (e.g., "I risk no more than 2% of my account per trade")
- You have a predefined stop loss (e.g., "If it drops below Y, I exit automatically")
- You have a predefined take-profit target (e.g., "I sell 50% at Z, let the rest run")
- You follow these rules regardless of how you feel

Systematic trading isn't sexy. It's boring. It means sitting on your hands while everyone else is making "easy money." It means taking small profits when you could have made more. It means cutting losses before you know if it would have recovered.

But it's the only thing that works long-term.
```

**Key features**:
1. Bulleted list for clarity
2. Examples in parentheses
3. Blunt reality check ("isn't sexy")
4. Short punch-line conclusion

## Instructions for Agents

### For WRITER

**When writing new chapters**:
- Open with personal story (specific, real failure)
- Use exact numbers ($, time, quantities)
- Follow setup-setup-punch rhythm
- Include "uncomfortable truths" sections
- Balance personal narrative (30%) with frameworks (40%) and concepts (30%)
- End sections with blunt summaries

**Checklist**:
- [ ] Opened with specific personal example?
- [ ] Used real dollar amounts and times?
- [ ] Included "Let me be clear" or similar direct address?
- [ ] Balanced confession with teaching?
- [ ] Avoided hype language and false promises?
- [ ] Ended with transition to next section/chapter?

### For EDITOR

**When editing**:
- DO NOT soften the blunt language
- DO NOT remove sentence fragments used for emphasis
- DO NOT add hype or promises
- DO NOT remove specific dollar amounts
- DO strengthen contrasts (emotional vs systematic)
- DO maintain first-person confessional tone

**Red flags** (when NOT to edit):
- Sentence fragments after emotional moments ("Gone.")
- Exact dollar/time amounts (even if they seem too specific)
- Blunt statements about losing money
- Personal failure stories (don't soften them)
- "This isn't X. It's Y." constructions

### For PROOFREADER

**When proofreading**:
- Check this file BEFORE "correcting" fragments
- Distinguish errors from intentional style
- Verify number consistency but don't question specificity
- Maintain em-dash usage (frequent, intentional)

**Intentional "errors" to preserve**:
1. Sentence fragments for emphasis
2. Starting sentences with "And" / "But"
3. Heavy em-dash usage
4. Lists without full sentence structure
5. Rhetorical questions without formal punctuation

## Notes

This voice is built on **credibility through vulnerability**. The author gains trust not by claiming expertise, but by honestly sharing expensive mistakes. The specific dollar amounts, times, and personal details make the failures (and eventually successes) feel real.

The rhythm—setup, setup, punch—keeps readers engaged while delivering harsh truths. The contrast between emotional trading stories and systematic frameworks gives the book both narrative drive and practical value.

**CRITICAL**: Do NOT soften this voice to make it more "professional" or less blunt. The directness is the differentiator. This book succeeds by being the anti-hype crypto book.
